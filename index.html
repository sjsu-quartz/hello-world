QuArtz

Queer Arts Programming, San Jose State University

QuArtz is an event series featuring presentations and workshops by visiting LGBTQ+ artists. All are welcome, supported by the SJSU PRIDE Center.

Edie Fake

November 19th, 2019

Edie Fake’s paintings and comics are grounded in issues of queer identity, community and describing trans embodiment through visual metaphor. Drawing from vernacular architecture and queer adornment, Fake's recent work expands the intimate scale of his paintings to full wall murals, including a new installation at the Berkeley Art Museum titled "Affordable Housing For Trans Elders."

Jamil Hellu

Tuesday October 29th, 2019

Jamil is a visual artist based in San Francisco, working primarily with photography and video installation. His ongoing portraiture project "Hues" explores intersectional connection of racial, gender, and sexual identities while seeking to stress contrasting manifestations of queer heritage and sexuality.

Indira Allegra

Thursday May 2nd, 2019

Indira Allegra works with tension as creative material to investigate themes of haunting and memorial. She is active in a range of fields including sculpture, performance, writing and installation.

Marcela Pardo Ariza

Wednesday April 10th, 2019

Marcela Pardo Ariza’s photographs incorporate quotidian objects in seemingly absurd ways, mixing recognizable elements with magical realism. Ariza’s visually provoking portraits seek to explore metaphors regarding race and gender through the action of looking within the theatricality of “the set.”

Mia Nakano

Wednesday March 20th, 2019

For over a decade, Mia Nakano has documented the lived experience of Queer/Trans Asian Pacific Americans through portraiture and personal narratives in the Visibility Project and ResilienceArchives. Her work advocates strategic and ethical use of visual arts to make social change.

Juan Carlos Rodríguez Rivera

Thursday February 7th, 2019

Juan Carlos Rodríguez Rivera discusses his work in decolonizing aesthetics of art and design. Born and raised in the smallest town of Puerto Rico, his work challenges colonial perspectives from the point of view of a boricua diaspora.
